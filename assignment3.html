Critique of the Spartacus Gay Travel Index

The Spartacus Gay Travel Index (link) visualization uses color-coded maps to display the safety of LGBTQ+ travel destinations worldwide and within the United States. It provides important insights for LGBTQ+ travelers, though several areas can be improved to enhance its overall effectiveness.

Strengths:

  • Usefulness: The visualization highlights general safety ratings of countries and U.S. states, which is valuable information for LGBTQ+ travelers. However, it lacks detailed context about specific risks or policies that influence these scores, earning it a usefulness rating of 5. Without these specifics, travelers may not fully understand the reasons behind a destination’s safety rating.
  • Completeness: The chart includes historical data, allowing viewers to track changes over time and potentially infer the broader social climate in each region. Yet, they do not capture all crucial details, such as specific safe regions, recent events, or policies, resulting in a completeness rating of 6. This lack of specific information limits travelers from making well-informed decisions based on the most current factors affecting safety.
  • Perceptibility: The maps are easy to understand at a basic level, but their reliance on a simple color scale and vague point system limits deeper understanding, resulting in a perceptibility rating of 5. The absence of clear categories or explanations means that viewers can see trends but cannot easily interpret what specific changes or points mean in real terms.

Areas for Improvement:

  • Truthfulness: With a rating of 3, the visualization lacks immediate sources, clear explanations of the point system, and specific data to verify safety ratings, making it difficult to assess accuracy. The absence of references or citations can leave viewers uncertain about the data's reliability.
  • Intuitiveness: The absence of a detailed legend or key makes it challenging for viewers to understand how scores are calculated, leading to a rating of 4 for intuitiveness. Without this information, users may misinterpret the ratings or fail to grasp the reasons behind them.
  • Aesthetics: The use of highly saturated colors and stark contrasts makes the visualization visually harsh, earning it an aesthetics rating of 4. This visual style can be distracting and make the data harder to interpret, rather than drawing users into the information.
  • Engagement: The charts offer some interactive features like hover functionality, but these add limited depth, reflected in an engagement rating of 4. The hover tool primarily repeats information available elsewhere (like the scroll bar above the map), lacking meaningful interactivity that would keep users engaged or prompt further exploration.

Recommendations for Improvement:

  • Add a detailed legend or key to clarify the point ranges, helping users understand the safety ratings better and providing context for why certain regions are rated as they are.
  • Include specific data sources: Providing references or links to each country’s policy updates, legal changes, or relevant incidents would add transparency and allow users to verify the data themselves, boosting confidence in the accuracy of the information.
  • Adjust the color scheme: Using softer gradients and more accessible color choices would improve visual appeal and accessibility for all viewers, making the data easier to look at and interpret, especially for those sensitive to high-contrast colors.
  • Enhance accessibility: Incorporate accessible design elements such as readable fonts, alt text, and image descriptions to make the visualization more inclusive, ensuring it can be understood and used by a wider audience, including those with visual impairments or color blindness.

Process: Redesign and Iterations

Moving forward, I began my first approach to redesigning the chart, focusing specifically on the map of the U.S. to keep the range narrow and focused. I considered several changes: softening the color gradient, updating the title to focus on U.S. states, adding sources for the information, and including specific data points based on the original Spartacus categories (i.e., anti-discrimination laws, marriage equality, sodomy laws, pride parade bans, and hate crimes). My goal was to retain the scale of the original chart while enhancing the information presented and making it more visually appealing.

The feedback I received suggested narrowing the focus even further, possibly highlighting specific cities that LGBTQ+ travelers might want to visit. Additionally, feedback recommended distinguishing between states to visit and states to avoid, refining the visualization’s focus to better serve its audience.

For the second iteration, I explored using a radar chart and developed two variations: one that displayed information on a city-by-city basis and another that combined multiple cities into a single radar chart, allowing users to filter specific cities via the legend. Ideally, hovering over the categories would provide simple score breakdowns, and filtering by city would make it more clear. The feedback was mainly positive, highlighting that focusing on cities LGBTQ+ travelers might consider for vacation was a practical approach. Suggestions included narrowing down to popular destinations or major cities. Another recommendation was to adjust the parameters to reflect factors more relevant to travel decisions. For instance, instead of including marriage equality, it would be more useful to highlight aspects like LGBTQ+ bars and nightlife, as vacationers are unlikely to choose a destination specifically for marriage laws.


I chose a radar chart for its ability to provide a more qualitative comparison between specific “destination” cities, unlike the original map that depicted broader geographic safety ratings. The radar chart offers a detailed view of multiple factors, making it easier to compare cities based on categories that are most relevant to LGBTQ+ travelers, such as LGBTQ+ nightlife (i.e., bars and clubs), anti-discrimination protections, LGBTQ+ community events (i.e., pride parades and festivals), safety or crime rates against LGBTQ+ individuals, and LGBTQ+ aligned businesses. The point system was expanded to a 10-point scale to allow travelers to better gauge the relative strengths and weaknesses of each location across various categories.

In designing the radar chart, I used straight lines instead of curved ones to more accurately represent the values. A simple color palette was chosen to avoid assumptions often associated with colors (i.e., red as negative or green as positive) and to ensure clarity, along with a color-blind safe scheme. A sans serif font was used to enhance readability, particularly for those with dyslexia.

To make the chart accessible, additional text was added to clarify the information and viewers can filter out cities and see individual scores on hover. The provided sources also offer an easily accessible way for users to explore more information about each city.
